Section 1.1Scope & Purpose

What NFPA 25 Is — & Why It Exists

§1.1 — Scope
NFPA 25 establishes the minimum requirements for the periodic inspection, testing, and maintenance of water-based fire protection systems. NFPA 13 / 14 / 20 get it built — NFPA 25 keeps it working.
Historical Catalyst
One Meridian Plaza fire — Philadelphia, 1991. Standpipe pressure-reducing valves were set wrong: firefighters couldn't get water, three died, eight floors burned. The system was installed — it just wasn't maintained and verified.
🎯Installation standards build it. NFPA 25 proves it's ready the day you need it.

Section 3.3The Three Core Words

Inspection vs. Testing vs. Maintenance

Inspection
§3.3.24
A visual examination to verify the system appears to be in operating condition and free of physical damage.
Look
Testing
§3.3.48
A procedure to determine operational status — waterflow, fire pump, alarm, and trip tests.
Operate
Maintenance
§3.3.27
Work performed to keep equipment operable over the life of the system.
Keep it running
📌Bonus: Repair (§3.3.40) = restore to sound working condition after damage — a separate concept from maintenance.

Sections 3.2–3.3The Distinction That Changes Your Duty

Deficiency vs. Impairment

Deficiency §3.3.8
A condition that will or could adversely affect performance — but does NOT rise to an impairment. The system still works; it's degraded.
▸ Critical (§3.3.8.1) — material effect in a fire
▸ Noncritical (§3.3.8.2) — no material effect, still fix it
Impairment §3.3.22
System or portion is OUT OF ORDER and would not function in a fire event. "Out of order" is the trigger word.
▸ Emergency (§3.3.22.1) — unplanned / found during ITM
▸ Preplanned (§3.3.22.2) — out of service for work
AHJ §3.2.2
The office or individual enforcing the standard.
Qualified §3.3.36
Competent, trained, acceptable to the AHJ.

Section 3.7The ITM Cycle · Foundations

ITM Frequency Windows — What "Annual" Really Means

Every frequency has a legal minimum and maximum. Miss the window, miss the code.
Weekly
Once per calendar week
§3.7.1.2
Monthly
Once per calendar month
§3.7.1.3
Quarterly
4×/year — min 2 / max 4 months apart
§3.7.1.4
Semiannual
2×/year — min 4 / max 8 months apart
§3.7.1.5
Annual
1×/year — min 9 / max 15 months apart
§3.7.1.6
3 Years
Every 36 months — min 30 / max 40
§3.7.1.7
5 Years
Every 60 months — min 54 / max 66
§3.7.1.8
⚠️An "annual" at 14 months is compliant — a "quarterly" 5 months apart is a violation. The window is the code.

Section 4.1.1Chapter 4 · General Requirements

Owner Responsibility

▸§4.1.1 — the property owner or designated representative is responsible for properly maintaining the system.
▸§4.1.1.2 — ITM shall be performed by qualified personnel.
▸§4.1.1.3 — where the owner is not the occupant, ITM & impairment authority may be delegated to a designated representative.
▸§4.1.1.4 — the designated representative must comply with all owner requirements in the standard.
▸§4.1.3 — owner must provide ready access to components that require ITM.
🔑You can delegate the work — the responsibility stays at the top.

Section 4.1.2Freeze Protection Is an Owner Duty

Keep the Water Above 40°F

▸§4.1.2 — buildings with water-filled piping kept at ≥ 40°F (4°C) unless antifreeze or dry system.
▸Heat-traced piping maintained per its listing — protect from freezing.
New in 2020 — §4.1.2.5
Enclosures around dry pipe, preaction, and deluge valves inspected daily during cold weather for adequate heat — weekly where a low-temperature alarm is installed (§4.1.2.5.1).
❄️Field note: a frozen valve enclosure is exactly how a "dry" system floods a building.

Section 4.1.4Before You Touch the System

Notification Before Testing or Shutdown

The AHJ
Authority having jurisdiction.
Fire Dept.
If required by the AHJ.
Alarm Facility
The monitoring company.
▸§4.1.4.1 — state the purpose, the system/component involved, and estimated time / expected duration.
▸§4.1.4.2 — notify the same parties again when the system is restored.
📞Not just for impairments — routine TESTING triggers the notification too. Make the calls first.

Sections 4.1.6–4.1.7When the Building Changes

Change the Hazard, Re-Evaluate the System

▸§4.1.6 — owner shall not change occupancy, use/process, or stored materials without evaluating the system's capability to protect the new condition.
▸§4.1.6.1 — this evaluation is NOT part of normal ITM — it's a separate engineering evaluation.
▸§4.1.7 — where changes are found, promptly evaluate adequacy and make approved corrections.
Trigger Examples — §4.1.6.2
Conversion to warehousing · relocated walls / mezzanines · removal of heating · storage height or commodity changes · water supply changes.
🏭Routine ITM proves it works as designed — not whether the design still fits what's in the building now.

Sections 4.1.5 / 4.1.8Corrections · Recalls · Signage

Fix It, Flag Recalls, Label the Valves

▸§4.1.5 — owner shall correct/repair deficiencies and impairments; work by qualified personnel or a qualified contractor (§4.1.5.2).
▸New 2020 — §4.1.5.1.1 — inspector notifies the owner in writing of recalled components; owner must remedy them (§4.1.5.1.2).
▸§4.1.8 — the location of shutoff valves shall be identified at the system riser or other approved location.
▸§4.1.9 — rigid information signs required at risers for antifreeze, dry, preaction, and auxiliary systems.
🏷️Little signs, big deal — they're how the next tech knows what they're walking up to.

Section 4.3Records — The 5 Required Elements

Every ITM Record Must Indicate Five Things

The procedure or activity performed
2
The organization that performed it
3
The required frequency
4
The results and the date
5
Name + contact info of the qualified contractor or owner (incl. lead person)
Life of the System
As-builts, hydraulic calcs, original acceptance tests, mfr data sheets (§4.3.4).
1 Year After the Next One
All subsequent ITM records (§4.3.5). Available to AHJ on request (§4.3.3).

Section 4.6Testing Obligations

Prove It Functions as Intended

▸§4.6.1 — all components and systems shall be tested to verify they function as intended.
▸§4.6.4 — compare results to the original acceptance test AND the most recent test — that's how you see degradation.
▸§4.6.5 — when a component is adjusted, repaired, reconditioned, or replaced, test it per the original acceptance test for that subsystem.
▸New 2020 — §4.6.6 — automated inspection/testing permitted: same action as a manual test, failures signal trouble/supervisory per NFPA 72.
🔧Fixing the hardware isn't enough — you must prove the subsystem works at the acceptance level.

Sections 4.4 / 4.1.11Don't Leave a Building Unprotected

Supplies Stay in Service — Impairments Get Tagged

▸§4.4 — during ITM, water supplies including fire pumps stay in service — unless under constant attendance by qualified personnel, or Chapter 15 procedures are followed.
▸§4.1.11 — where a system is impaired, follow Chapter 15 procedures and attach a tag.
▸§4.1.11.2 — on return to service, verify the system works before you walk away.
🚫Never walk away from an unattended, un-tagged, out-of-service supply. (Deep dive in Section 8.)
