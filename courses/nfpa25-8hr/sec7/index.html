Chapter 14The Invisible Threat

Why Internal Condition Matters

▸A system can be fully charged, every head in place, gauges normal — and still be starving for water inside.
▸Scale, tubercules, and debris build up where you can't see them.
▸The outside of the pipe tells you nothing about the inside.
🔬A system can pass every visual inspection and still be badly obstructed. Chapter 14 makes you look inside.

Section 14.2Precise 2020 Language

The 5-Year Internal Assessment

▸§14.2.1 — conduct an assessment of the internal condition of piping, inspecting for foreign organic and inorganic material.
▸§14.2.1.1 — at a minimum, every 5 years. OR —
▸§14.2.1.2 — at a frequency determined by an approved risk analysis.
▸§14.2.1.5 — nonmetallic piping is exempt.
Note — 2020 Wording Changed
The old "open a flushing connection + remove a sprinkler" method moved to the annex as guidance. Mandatory text just requires the assessment.

Section 14.2.2Saves Labor — Until It Doesn't

Multiple Wet Systems? Alternate Them

▸§14.2.2 — in a building with multiple wet pipe systems, every other system gets the assessment each cycle.
▸§14.2.2.1 — the skipped systems are assessed at the next frequency.
The Catch — §14.2.2.2
If foreign material is found in any one system, then ALL systems in the building must be assessed.
⚠️One dirty riser condemns the whole building to a full look.

Sections 14.2.1.3–4The Assessment Is the Trigger

What You Find — & What It Triggers

You Find
Pipe scale · rust · sludge · foreign objects · zebra mussels · and the two red flags: tubercules & slime.
It Triggers
Tubercules or slime → test for MIC (§14.2.1.3).
Enough material to obstruct → full obstruction investigation per §14.3 (§14.2.1.4).
🔀The assessment is the trigger — the investigation is the deep dive.

Obstruction Investigation Triggers (1–8)

6Plugged sprinklers

Section 14.3.3When the Investigation Confirms It

The Flushing Program

▸§14.3.3 — if the investigation indicates sufficient material to obstruct pipe or sprinklers, a complete flushing program is conducted by qualified personnel.
▸Not a quick rinse — a complete, engineered program.
Where the Procedures Live
The detailed flushing procedures — flow rates, hydraulic vs. hydropneumatic methods — are in Annex D of NFPA 25.
💧After flushing: verify flow is restored, document, and set the re-inspection schedule.

MICThe Villain Behind Internal Corrosion

Microbiologically Influenced Corrosion

▸Corrosion driven by colonies of bacteria living in the system water.
▸Produces tubercules, slime, pinhole leaks, and blockages — attacks from the inside out.
▸Thrives in stagnant, untreated water — exactly what sits in a lot of sprinkler piping.
The Code Hook — MANDATORY
Find tubercules or slime — during the assessment (§14.2.1.3) or an obstruction investigation (§14.3.4) — and you test for MIC. Not optional.

MICRecognize It on the Job

Spotting MIC in the Field

▸Black or slimy deposits inside the pipe
▸Rotten-egg (sulfide) smell when draining
▸Pinhole leaks in otherwise sound pipe
▸Crusty tubercule mounds over pits
▸Accelerated, localized corrosion at joints/welds
▸Discolored water at drain tests
🧫Confirm by lab sampling — then treatment, and often pipe replacement.

Section 14.4A Cold-Storage Hazard

Ice Obstruction — Refrigerated Spaces

▸§14.4 — dry or preaction piping through a refrigerated space below 32°F (0°C): internally inspected for ice annually, at the point of entry to the refrigerated area.
▸§14.4.2 — ice found → examine additional piping to find how far it extends.
▸Alternative nondestructive methods permitted (§14.4.1).
🧊Ice plugs form where moist system air meets cold pipe — that's why air dryness matters in freezers.

FieldThe Paperwork That Closes It Out

Document It — & Know When It's an Impairment

▸Record assessment findings, investigation results, and flushing per §4.3 — the 5 required record elements.
▸Material found in one system → all systems assessed (§14.2.2.2).
▸Obstructed enough that the system can't perform = impairment → Chapter 15 + tag.
▸Set the re-inspection clock: 5 years, or per approved risk analysis.
