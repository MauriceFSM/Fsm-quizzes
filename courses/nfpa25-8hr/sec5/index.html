Chapter 6Classes Defined in NFPA 14 · Demands Set in NFPA 25

Standpipe Classes & Test Demands

Class I
2½" · FIRE DEPARTMENT
Demand (§6.3.1.2): 500 gpm most remote + 250 gpm each additional standpipe.
Class II
1½" · OCCUPANT USE
Demand (§6.3.1.3): 100 gpm at the most remote hose connection.
Class III
BOTH
Serves both fire dept. and occupants — same demand basis as Class I.
🎯Class I / III = 500 + 250 gpm. Class II = 100 gpm at the most remote connection.

Section 6.3.1Proving the Supply Still Delivers

The 5-Year Flow Test

▸§6.3.1.1 — every 5 years, flow test all automatic standpipe systems: verify required flow & pressure at the hydraulically most remote hose valve outlet while flowing full demand.
▸§6.3.1.1.1 — where the remote outlet isn't practical, the AHJ approves an alternate test location.
▸§6.3.1.4 — demand is based on the design criteria in effect at installation.
▸§6.3.1.6 — a main drain test is also required on each standpipe with an automatic supply.
🌊Five-year flow test at the most remote outlet, at full system demand.

Section 6.3.2Only Certain Systems Need It

The 5-Year Hydrostatic Test

▸§6.3.2.1 — every 5 years, hydrostatically test manual standpipes and semiautomatic dry standpipes — including the FDC piping — at 200 psi for 2 hours (or 50 psi above max where max exceeds 150 psi).
▸§6.3.2.1.1 — exemption: manual-wet standpipes in a combined sprinkler/standpipe system don't require the hydro.
▸§6.3.2.2 — pressure measured at the low elevation point of the system.
Field Tip — §6.3
Where water damage is possible, run an air test at 25 psi first before introducing water.
🧪An automatic wet standpipe proves itself daily under pressure — that's why only manual & dry get the hydro.

Section 6.3.1.5Handled by Chapter 13

Standpipe PRVs & Waterflow Alarms

▸§6.3.1.5 — standpipe pressure-reducing & regulating valves are inspected, tested, and maintained per Chapter 13.
▸Hose-connection PRVs (§13.5.2): partial flow annually, full flow every 5 years.
▸§6.3.3 — waterflow & supervisory alarm devices test per Chapter 13.
Why This Matters — One Meridian Plaza, 1991
Standpipe PRVs set to the wrong pressure left firefighters with useless hose streams — three died. This is the reason PRV testing exists. Do not skip it.

Chapter 7The Yard Piping That Feeds the Whole Site

Private Fire Service Mains — Overview

What Chapter 7 Covers
The private underground and exposed piping on a property that carries water from the source out to hydrants, monitor nozzles, and the sprinkler & standpipe risers — everything between the public supply and the building.
Hose Maintenance
Fire hose on these systems is maintained per NFPA 1962 (§7.1.4) — same standard as standpipe hose.
🗺️Think of the yard loop — source → underground main → hydrants & risers across the site.

Section 7.2.2.1Inspected Very Differently

Piping — Exposed vs. Underground

Exposed §7.2.2.1.1
Inspected annually for: ① leaks ② physical damage ③ corrosion ④ restraint methods.
▸ Inaccessible piping inspected during each scheduled shutdown
Underground §7.3.1
Can't be visually inspected — its condition is proven by the flow test.
▸ Exposed you look at; underground you flow
🔍Exposed piping = annual visual. Underground = proven by flow test.

Section 7.3.1Finding the Buried Problem

The 5-Year Main Flow Test

▸§7.3.1 — underground AND exposed piping flow tested at minimum 5-year intervals.
▸§7.3.1.1 — deteriorated results are investigated to the AHJ's satisfaction.
▸§7.3.1.2 — where a full flow test isn't possible, test at the maximum flow available.
▸§7.2.2.3 — mainline strainers cleaned after significant flow; removed & inspected annually.
🕳️The flow test is how you catch a main that's tuberculated or partially closed underground.

Sections 7.2.2.4–5Annually & After Each Operation

Hydrants — Inspection

▸Dry-barrel & wall hydrants (§7.2.2.4) and wet-barrel (§7.2.2.5): inspected annually AND after each operation.
▸Dry-barrel key check: water or ice in the barrel → faulty drain, leaky valve, or high groundwater.
▸Also: cracks, outlet & thread condition, operating nut, wrench present.
❄️Water in a dry-barrel hydrant going into winter = a hydrant-shaped block of ice in January. That drain has to work.

Sections 7.3.2 / 7.4.2Test It · Drain It · Grease It

Hydrants — Annual Test & Maintenance

▸§7.3.2 — tested annually: open fully, flow until foreign material clears, flow at least 1 minute (§7.3.2.2).
▸§7.3.2.4 — dry-barrel & wall hydrants must fully drain within 60 minutes.
▸§7.3.2.5 — won't drain / high groundwater → plug and pump out; mark for pumping in freezing climates.
▸§7.4.2.1 — lubricated annually; kept clear of snow & ice with free access (§7.4.2.2).
🔧Annual test · 1-minute flow · 60-minute drain · annual lube.

Sections 7.2.2.7–8Two More Chapter 7 Items

Monitor Nozzles & Hose Houses

Monitor Nozzles §7.2.2.7
Inspected semiannually — leakage, damage, corrosion.
▸ Oscillated through full range annually (§7.3.3.2)
▸ Lubricated annually
Hose Houses §7.2.2.8
Inspected quarterly — accessible, undamaged, all equipment present.
💦Monitor nozzles = semiannual (full range annual). Hose houses = quarterly.

FieldWhat You Actually Find

Common Standpipe & Main Deficiencies

▸Missing caps / plugs on hose connections & FDCs
▸Seized hose valves — not operated in years
▸Outdated hose service test
▸Obstructed or damaged FDC
▸Buried / overgrown / blocked hydrants
▸Dry-barrel hydrant holding water
▸Missing or illegible hydraulic design sign
▸Flow test past due — supply unverified
📋Every one of these is a documented finding.

FieldWhen a Test Comes Back Bad

Failed Test? Corrective Action & Documentation

▸Investigate low flow / low residual against previous test results — find what changed.
▸A main or standpipe that can't meet demand = impairment → Chapter 15 procedures + tag.
▸Document per §4.3 — the five required record elements from Section 1.
▸Notify the owner in writing.
🚩A failed test isn't the end of the job — it's the start of the corrective-action paperwork.
