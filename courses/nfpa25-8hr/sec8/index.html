Section 4.1.1The Records Side Begins

Who's Responsible for the Records

▸§4.1.1 — the property owner or designated representative is ultimately responsible — including maintaining the records.
▸Not the fire department, not the contractor. The contractor performs the work and hands over documentation; the owner keeps it.
▸New 2020 — §4.3.1.1 — makes it explicit: records maintained by the property owner.
🔑The buck stops with the owner — same principle you learned in Section 1.

Section 4.3.2Know Them — and Know What Doesn't Belong

The Five Required Record Elements

1
The procedure or activity performed
2
The organization that performed it
3
The required frequency
4
The results and the date
5
Name + contact info of the qualified contractor or owner (incl. lead person)
🚫NOT required: the cost of the inspection, the weather, or the insurance carrier.

Sections 4.3.4–4.3.5Two Retention Buckets

How Long You Keep Records

Life of the System
Permanent records (§4.3.4): as-builts, hydraulic calcs, original acceptance test documents, manufacturer data sheets.
As long as the system exists, these documents exist.
1 Year After the Next
Routine ITM records (§4.3.5): kept until one year after the next activity of that same type.
A quarterly report lives until 1 year after the next quarterly.
🗂️Permanent = life of the system. Routine = 1 year past the next one.

Sections 4.1.4 / 4.6.5Two Rules That Tie Into Impairment

Notifications & Test-After-Repair

▸§4.1.4 — before shutting down or testing, notify the AHJ, the fire department (if required), and the alarm-receiving facility.
▸A shutdown is often an impairment — so these notifications matter again here.
▸§4.6.5 — whenever a component is adjusted, repaired, reconditioned, or replaced, it must be tested. Don't just document — prove the fix works.
🔧Repair, then test. Documentation alone isn't enough.

Chapter 15The Core of This Section

Impairments — The Big Picture

▸An impairment is a condition where the system (or part) is out of service and would not work in a fire.
▸Chapter 15 is the structured process so a building is never quietly left unprotected.
▸Applies whether planned (you're doing work) or unplanned (broken pipe, failed component).
🛡️One question drives the whole chapter: how do we keep people safe while the sprinklers are off?

Section 15.2The Single Point of Accountability

The Impairment Coordinator

▸§15.2 — the owner assigns an impairment coordinator.
▸If none is designated, the owner is the coordinator by default (§15.2.2).
▸Where a lease grants ITM authority to a tenant / management firm, they assign one (§15.2.3).
▸The coordinator authorizes the impairment, makes notifications, and ensures protective measures are in place.
👤Every impairment has a coordinator — the person accountable for it.

Section 15.3Make It Impossible to Miss

Tag the System

▸§15.3 — a tag is used to indicate a system, or part of a system, has been removed from service.
▸Posted at the system control valve, the fire department connection, and any AHJ-required locations.
▸Its job: make the out-of-service condition impossible to miss — so no one assumes protection is available when it isn't.
🏷️Any time a system or part is removed from service → it gets tagged. No exceptions.

Section 15.5You Know About It In Advance

Preplanned Impairments

▸§15.5 — all preplanned impairments are authorized by the impairment coordinator BEFORE the impairment begins.
▸The coordinator then works the checklist: extent & duration · inspect the areas involved · make the notifications.
▸Notify: the fire department, insurance carrier, alarm company, AHJ, and the building occupants & supervisors.
📋Nothing goes out of service on a planned basis until the coordinator authorizes it.

Section 15.5A Number Worth Memorizing

The 10-Hour Rule

▸§15.5 — if a system will be out of service more than 10 hours in a 24-hour period, take an additional protective measure.
Evacuate
the affected area
Fire Watch
an approved fire watch
Temp Supply
temporary water supply
Control Sources
approved ignition/fuel control
⏰Under 10 hours → tags & notifications. Over 10 hours → actively protect the space.

Section 15.6Unplanned — But No Shortcuts

Emergency Impairments

▸Unplanned: a ruptured or frozen pipe, interrupted water supply, failed component, or an impairment found during your own ITM work.
▸§15.6 — the coordinator implements the same steps as a preplanned impairment (§15.5).
▸Same tag, same notifications, same protective measures, same restoration.
🚨No shortened checklist — you run the full Chapter 15 process, just starting when you discover it.

Section 15.7Closing Out the Impairment

Restoring a System to Service

1
Verify the necessary inspections & tests confirm the system works
2
Notify FD, insurance carrier, alarm company, AHJ, occupants & supervisors that protection is back
3
Remove the impairment tag — the final step
✅The tag stays on until the system is verified back in service — removing it is the last act.
