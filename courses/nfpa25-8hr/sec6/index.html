Chapter 8Flow & Pressure When the Supply Can't Deliver

Why Fire Pumps Matter

▸A fire pump boosts pressure and flow when the supply can't meet system demand on its own.
▸Rated in GPM (flow) and PSI (pressure) — the net pressure it adds.
Churn
NO FLOW
Pump running against a closed system.
100%
RATED CAPACITY
The pump's rated design point.
150%
OVERLOAD
Peak demand — proves reserve.
📈Those three points draw the pump curve — remember them for the annual flow test.

Section 8.3.1The Exam Favorite

No-Flow (Churn) Test — How Often?

Diesel §8.3.1.1.1
WEEKLY — period.
Electric §8.3.1.2.2
MONTHLY — the default baseline.
BUT Electric = WEEKLY for these 4 (§8.3.1.2.1)
① Beyond the fire department's pumping capacity · ② limited-service controllers · ③ vertical turbine pumps · ④ suction from a ground-level tank or a source without useful pressure. A redundant fire pump allows monthly even for these (§8.3.1.2.3).
⚙️Diesel weekly · electric monthly — except the 4 categories, which go weekly.

Section 8.3.2Prove It Starts and Holds Pressure

Running the Churn Test

▸§8.3.2.2 — start the pump automatically (drop pressure on the sensing line — auto-start is what you're proving).
▸§8.3.2.3 — electric pump runs a minimum of 10 minutes.
▸§8.3.2.4 — diesel pump runs a minimum of 30 minutes (needs the run to reach operating temp).
▸§8.3.2.7 — qualified personnel in attendance (unless approved remote automated testing, 5-min response).
⏱️Run times: electric 10 min · diesel 30 min.

Sections 8.3.2.8–9Gathering the Data

What You Record & Check

▸Record suction & discharge gauge readings — idle and running.
▸Pump packing glands: a slight discharge is correct — adjust gland nuts if needed.
▸Check for unusual noise or vibration; packing boxes, bearings, casing for overheating.
▸Circulation relief valve discharging a small flow at churn (§8.3.2.1.1.1).
▸New 2020 — record controller event-log high/low pressures on electronic-sensor pumps.
🔧Packing that weeps a little is healthy — bone dry means it's too tight.

Section 8.2.2Before You Even Run the Pump

The Weekly Pump Room Inspection

Heat: ≥ 40°F (4°C)
Pump room with an electric pump, or a diesel with an engine heater.
Heat: ≥ 70°F (21°C)
Pump room with a diesel WITHOUT an engine heater. Don't default to 40 for everything.
▸Ventilation louvers free · suction/discharge/bypass valves open · no leaks · pressures normal.
▸Electric: controller pilot light on, transfer-switch normal light on, reverse-phase alarm off.

Section 8.2.2(4)Diesels Have Extra Weekly Items

Diesel Weekly Checks & Fuel Care

▸Fuel tank at least two-thirds full · battery voltage & charging current in range.
▸Crankcase oil level · cooling water level · water-jacket heater (block heater) operating.
▸§8.3.4.1 — diesel fuel tested annually for degradation; if it fails, recondition/replace, clean tank, change filters, retest every 6 months.
▸§8.1.1.2 — engine oil, oil filter, fuel filter changed every 50 hours or annually.
⛽Keep that fuel tank ≥ two-thirds full — the number the quiz asks for.

Section 8.3.7.2A Common Misconception — Listen Closely

Pass or Fail? The 95% Rule

▸§8.3.7.2.3 — acceptable when net pressure at each flow point is ≥ 95% of one of: the original manufacturer's curve, the original unadjusted field acceptance curve, or a nameplate-generated curve.
The Key Word Is ORIGINAL
You are not comparing to last year's test — you compare to the pump's original performance.
📉§8.3.7.2.4 — below 95%? Notify the owner in writing & investigate. Can't meet demand = impairment.

Sections 8.3.3.12–13Only As Good As Its Controls

Controllers, Alarms & Transfer Switches

▸§8.3.3.13 — alarm conditions simulated annually at the sensors: pump running, loss of power, phase reversal.
▸§8.3.3.12 — transfer switch tested annually: simulate power failure at peak load, verify transfer, run ≥ 2 minutes on alternate power, verify transfer back.
▸§8.3.6.2 — automatic transfer switches also exercised routinely per NFPA 110.
▸2020 safety: arrange tasks so you're not opening an energized controller — NFPA 70E applies.
🔌Transfer switch: run at least 2 minutes on the alternate power source.

Section 9.2.5The Interval Depends on the Tank

Tank Interior Inspections

Every 3 Years §9.2.5.1.1
Steel tanks WITHOUT corrosion protection.
Every 5 Years §9.2.5.1.2
All other tanks — steel with corrosion protection, concrete, wood, fiberglass.
▸§9.2.5.3 — looking for pitting, corrosion, spalling, rot, debris, aquatic growth, coating failure; anti-vortex plate.
▸§9.2.6 — steel pitting/coating failure triggers dry-film-thickness, ultrasonic, and vacuum-box tests.

Sections 9.3 / 9.5Tests & Automatic Fill Valves

Tank Tests & Fill Valves

▸§9.3.1 — level indicators tested every 5 years for accuracy & free movement.
▸§9.3.5 — high and low water level signals tested annually.
▸§9.3.2–9.3.4 — heating system, low-water-temp signals, and high-temp limit switches tested before each heating season.
▸§9.5 — automatic tank fill valves tested annually (lower level, record refill); strainers cleaned quarterly.
❄️Freeze-protection tests happen before winter — so you know they work when it counts.
