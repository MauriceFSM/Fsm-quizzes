Start every rack problem by identifying the ceiling sprinkler type — that's your section. Then use aisle width + sprinkler temperature to find the correct curve.

2. The Three Inputs That Drive Every Rack Storage Design

  1. Rack type — single-row, double-row, or multiple-row → picks your table
  2. Aisle width — wider than 8 ft vs. 8 ft or narrower → picks your curve group
  3. Ceiling sprinkler temperature rating — ordinary vs. high temp → picks your exact curve
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: The critical aisle width split is 8 ft. Multiple-row racks with aisles >8 ft use different (less demanding) design curves than those with aisles ≤8 ft. Narrower aisles = fire spreads across faster = more demanding design.

3. In-Rack Sprinklers Reduce Ceiling Demand — The Key Trade-Off

Adding in-rack sprinklers lowers the required ceiling sprinkler density. More in-rack levels = bigger reduction. Example: double-row rack, 8 ft aisles, exposed non-encapsulated Class III, 24 ft storage, high-temp ceiling sprinklers:

In-Rack CoverageCeiling Density Required
One level of in-rack0.25 gpm/ft² / 2,000 ft²
In-rack at every tier0.15 gpm/ft² / 2,000 ft²
In-rack at every tier = ceiling system at its minimum (0.15 gpm/ft² floor). This is why retrofit jobs with limited water supply often add in-rack sprinklers — it dramatically reduces the overhead system demand.

5. Encapsulation Penalties in Rack Storage (§21.4)

For encapsulated commodities in rack storage over 12 ft:

  • Class I–III: ceiling density increased by 25%
  • Class IV: ceiling density increased by 50%
⚠️ Rack trigger = over 12 ft. Palletized/solid-piled encapsulation trigger = over 15 ft. These are different storage types with different threshold heights. The exam will try to get you to mix them up.

6. Horizontal Barriers — When and Why

A horizontal barrier is a solid barrier (sheet metal, wood, or similar) that spans the full length and depth of the rack at a specific height increment. Its job: stop vertical fire spread between rack tiers.

  • Must extend full length and depth of the rack
  • Must be strong enough not to sag during loading/unloading
  • Used in combination with in-rack sprinklers to compartmentalize vertical fire development
Where clearance to ceiling exceeds 10 ft with CMDA ceiling sprinklers protecting exposed non-encapsulated Class I–IV, a horizontal barrier should be installed above storage with one level of in-rack sprinklers under it (per Annex §A.25.3.3.1.1.6).

7. In-Rack Independent of Ceiling Sprinklers (§25.6)

Section 25.6 allows protection purely from inside the rack — no ceiling system involvement for rack protection. Key rules:

  • Closed-top commodities only (no open-top containers)
  • Wet pipe systems only
  • Minimum K-factor: K-8.0
  • Minimum in-rack spacing: 27 inches between heads
  • Single-row racks: minimum 6 in-rack sprinklers at 60 gpm
  • Double/multiple-row racks: minimum 8 in-rack sprinklers at 60 gpm
The top level of in-rack sprinklers is treated as a "floor" for ceiling system design purposes — ceiling system design follows Chapters 20–23 above that level.

9. Solid Shelf Racks — Always Need In-Rack Sprinklers

Solid shelves act like mini-ceilings inside the rack. They block water from ceiling sprinklers from reaching the lower storage levels. Result: in-rack sprinklers are mandatory at every tier with solid shelves, regardless of commodity class.

ESFR sprinklers are not permitted when solid shelves are present — unless the solid shelf racks are protected with in-rack sprinklers per §25.5.1.

⚠️ ESFR also cannot protect storage with open-top containers. Both solid shelves and open-tops block the ESFR discharge from reaching the fire interior.

10. Face Sprinklers — Rack Storage over 25 ft (Plastics)

For rack storage of Group A plastic commodities over 25 ft, face sprinklers are required. A face sprinkler is:

  • A standard sprinkler placed in the transverse flue space along the aisle or in the rack
  • Located within 18 inches of the aisle face of storage
  • Purpose: oppose vertical development of fire on the external face of storage
Face sprinklers are located within the rack structure — walkways are NOT considered flue spaces for face sprinkler placement purposes.
