The 5 Hydraulic Reference Points

  • Water Supply — source of all flow (city main, tank, pump)
  • System Riser — vertical pipe up the building; "base of riser" is the standard reference
  • Cross Main — horizontal pipe feeding the branch lines
  • Branch Lines — smaller pipes that sprinklers thread onto
  • Sprinklers — discharge devices, each protects a coverage area

Why this matters

Hydraulic calculations work backward — start at the most remote sprinkler, accumulate pressure losses through branch lines, cross main, and riser, end at the water supply. Knowing the component names is the bare minimum.

K-Factor — The Core Equation

Every sprinkler discharge calculation starts here

The Formula

Q = K √P
  • Q = flow in gpm (gallons per minute)
  • K = sprinkler discharge coefficient (the K-factor)
  • P = pressure at the sprinkler in psi

Bigger K = more flow at the same pressure. K is determined by the sprinkler's orifice size and is on the sprinkler nameplate.

K-Factor Cheat Sheet

K (US)K (metric)Typical Use
K-5.6K-80Standard LH/OH ceiling — most common
K-8.0K-115OH2, light storage, residential
K-11.2K-160Storage, CMSA, EH
K-14.0K-200Storage, ESFR
K-16.8K-240ESFR, high-challenge storage
K-22.4 / 25.2K-320 / K-360Largest ESFR for tall storage

Worked check

A K-5.6 sprinkler at 7 psi (the minimum operating pressure for most spray sprinklers) discharges: Q = 5.6 × √7 = 5.6 × 2.65 = 14.8 gpm. At 50 psi: Q = 5.6 × √50 = 39.6 gpm. Pressure quadruples → flow doubles.

Two Ways to Size a System

Pipe Schedule vs Hydraulic — Chapter 19 sets the rules

Method 1 — Pipe Schedule

Pipe sizes are selected from a lookup table based on the number of sprinklers each pipe must feed. No flow or pressure calculation required.

  • Simpler — count sprinklers, look up pipe size
  • Limited to LH and OH (and additions to existing EH)
  • Often oversized compared to hydraulic
  • Tables: 27.5.2.2.1 (LH steel) and 27.5.3.4 (OH steel)

→ §19.3.2, §27.5 — limited to LH/OH

Method 2 — Hydraulic Calculation

Every pipe section is sized by calculating actual flow and pressure loss from the most remote sprinkler back to the water supply.

  • Rigorous — uses Hazen-Williams + elevation + fittings
  • Required for EH, K-factors other than nominal 5.6, and unlisted piping materials
  • Optimizes pipe size — often cheaper construction
  • Generates the formal 4-sheet calc package for AHJ

→ §19.3.3, §27.2 — required for EH and most modern designs

§19.3.1.1 — the supersession rule: A hydraulically calculated system supersedes the pipe schedule rules in Chapter 27. Once you go hydraulic, you are no longer bound by the schedule limits.

When Pipe Schedule is Allowed

§19.3.2.3 — Three permitted use cases, and one big "no"

§19.3.2.3 — Permitted Uses

  1. Light and Ordinary Hazard occupancies — using pipe sizes from §27.5
  2. Additions or modifications to existing Extra Hazard pipe-schedule systems
  3. New systems of 5,000 ft² (465 m²) or less

Most LH/OH offices and retail spaces qualify for pipe schedule. It's the legacy path.

§27.5.1.2 — Pipe Schedule DOES NOT Apply When…

  • Extra Hazard Groups 1 & 2 (new systems)
  • K-factors other than nominal 5.6
  • Listed piping material not in Table 7.3.1.1
  • Antifreeze systems (separate hydraulic requirements)
  • Foam-water sprinkler systems

Any of these → hydraulic calc required.

In practice today: Most new designs are hydraulic anyway — software makes it cheap, and the resulting pipe is smaller (less material, easier install). Pipe schedule lives mostly in small LH retrofits and tenant fit-outs.

Density / Area Curve Concept

Figure 19.3.3.1.1 — Trading water intensity for area coverage

What the Curve Does

Each hazard tier has a downward-sloping curve on the density (gpm/ft²) vs area (ft²) chart.

  • Higher density → smaller area must be calculated
  • Lower density → larger area must be calculated
  • The total flow demand from sprinklers stays roughly equivalent across the curve

The design just needs to land on any single point on the curve (§19.3.3.2.1.2).

Curve Endpoints (Typical Starting Values)

HazardDensity startMin area
LH0.10 gpm/ft²1,500 ft²
OH10.15 gpm/ft²1,500 ft²
OH20.20 gpm/ft²1,500 ft²
EH10.30 gpm/ft²2,500 ft²
EH20.40 gpm/ft²2,500 ft²

EH minimums jump to 2,500 ft² — bigger fires need more sprinklers operating.

Why a curve, not a single point? Lower density over a larger area protects against an undetected fire that spreads. Higher density over a smaller area protects against a fast-growing fire that's caught quickly. The curve gives the designer flexibility — pick what fits the building.

Reading the Curve — Worked Example

An OH2 design — same building, two valid points

Option A — High Density, Smaller Area

  • Density: 0.20 gpm/ft²
  • Area: 1,500 ft² (minimum)
  • Total flow: 0.20 × 1,500 = 300 gpm (sprinklers only)
  • Sprinklers covering 130 ft² each → ~12 sprinklers

Best when ceiling is low and detection is fast.

Option B — Lower Density, Larger Area

  • Density: 0.15 gpm/ft²
  • Area: 3,900 ft² (the curve endpoint for OH2)
  • Total flow: 0.15 × 3,900 = 585 gpm (sprinklers only)
  • Sprinklers covering 130 ft² each → ~30 sprinklers

Lower density per sprinkler but many more sprinklers.

The Trade-off

Option A: 12 sprinklers at higher pressure, smaller pipes, lower total demand. Usually wins on cost.
Option B: 30 sprinklers at lower pressure each, bigger pipes, higher total demand. Useful when ceiling is high or pipe pressure is limited.
Both satisfy §19.3.3.2.1.2 — you only need ONE valid point on the curve.

The Single-Point Rule

§19.3.3.2.1.2 — You don't have to satisfy every point on the curve

The Rule

"The calculation must satisfy any single point on the appropriate curve. It is not required to meet every point simultaneously."

This is one of the most-misunderstood concepts in NFPA 13.

What It Means Practically

  • You pick the design density (your choice within the curve)
  • You pick the design area (your choice within the curve)
  • The COMBINATION you picked must land on or above the curve
  • You don't have to prove water for a different density/area combo

Why It Matters

A reviewer who insists "your 0.20 density doesn't satisfy what 0.15 over 3,900 would need" is misreading the code. Either combo is valid.

The Catch

Choosing the EASIEST point doesn't mean choosing the CHEAPEST. Run both density/area extremes if pipe sizing is borderline. Sometimes a higher density saves pipe.

Minimum Areas Rule

§19.3.3.1.4 — Floor of the curve

The Two Floors

The density/area curve cannot drop below certain minimum areas — even if higher density would mathematically support it.

1,500
ft² min · LH & OH
2,500
ft² min · EH 1 & 2

Why the Floor Exists

Fires don't politely stay in the area you calculated. The minimum area requires the system to handle a credible fire that could grow beyond a few sprinklers.

EH minimums are higher because:

  • EH fires grow faster
  • More sprinklers will operate before control is achieved
  • 2,500 ft² ≈ 16 to 20 sprinklers at typical 130 ft² coverage

Counter-example

You CAN'T design an OH2 system at 0.30 gpm/ft² over 1,000 ft². The math says 300 gpm total — same as 0.20 × 1,500. But §19.3.3.1.4 says the area can't drop below 1,500 ft². The rule isn't about flow, it's about credible fire size.

Dry Pipe Area Increase

§19.3.3.2.5 — Dry systems get penalized; here's why

The Rule

For dry pipe and double-interlock preaction systems, the design area shall be increased by 30%, without revising the density.

No exceptions for size or hazard — if it's dry or double-interlock, the area goes up.

Why the Penalty

Between sprinkler activation and water arrival, a dry system has a delay while air is exhausted. During that delay:

  • More sprinklers operate from heat
  • Fire spreads to nearby sprinklers
  • The effective "design area" enlarges

30% increase is the NFPA estimate of that effective enlargement.

Worked numbers

OH2 wet-pipe design: 0.20 gpm/ft² over 1,500 ft² = 300 gpm sprinkler demand.
Same OH2 as DRY pipe: 0.20 gpm/ft² over 1,500 × 1.30 = 1,950 ft² = 390 gpm sprinkler demand. 30% more water needed just because the system is dry.

Design implication: If you can use wet pipe (heated building, no freeze risk), do it. The 30% area penalty on dry pipe ripples through pipe sizing, water supply, and pump sizing.

High-Temp Sprinkler Reduction

§19.3.3.2.4 — High-temperature sprinklers earn a smaller design area in EH

The Rule

In Extra Hazard occupancies, when high-temperature sprinklers are used, the design area may be reduced by 25%.

Floor: the reduced area shall not be less than 2,000 ft².

Only applies to EH — not LH/OH.

Why High-Temp Earns It

High-temperature sprinklers (286°F vs the standard 165°F) resist opening from heat — so fewer sprinklers operate around the fire's edges before the design area is reached.

The trade-off: a slightly slower response, but more sprinklers stay closed to deliver flow where it counts.

Stacking with dry pipe

EH1 dry pipe with high-temp: start at 2,500 ft² base → ×1.30 (dry) = 3,250 ft² → ×0.75 (high-temp) = 2,437 ft². The 30% increase and 25% reduction don't cancel cleanly — they multiply. And they cannot drop below 2,000 ft².

MODULE 5 · §27.2.2.5SLIDE 30 / 60FSM

Sprinkler Flow — Two Equations Together

Density × coverage tells you what you need; Q = K√P tells you what pressure delivers it

Equation 1 — Required Flow

Q_required = density × A_cov

Each sprinkler must deliver at least this flow to meet the density target across its coverage area.

Example — OH2 at 130 ft² coverage:

Q_req = 0.20 × 130 = 26 gpm

Equation 2 — Pressure to Deliver It

Q = K √P → P = (Q/K)²

For a K-5.6 sprinkler delivering 26 gpm:

P = (26 / 5.6)² = 4.64² = 21.5 psi

If your starting pressure is below 21.5 psi at the most remote sprinkler, density is not met.

Why both equations matter

Density × area gives you a flow target. K-factor + pressure tells you whether that flow is actually achievable at the available pressure. If a sprinkler needs 26 gpm but only 7 psi is available, you're not delivering 26 gpm — you're delivering 14.8 gpm and the design fails. Both equations must balance at every sprinkler.

§27.2.2.5 — flow calculation rule: The discharge flow used in calculations is determined from the calculated pressure, not the minimum 7 psi. If pressure builds up upstream, flow increases accordingly.

Hazen-Williams — The Core Equation

§27.2.2.1.1 — Friction loss in every pipe segment

The Formula

p = 4.52 × Q¹·⁸⁵ / (C¹·⁸⁵ × d⁴·⁸⁷)
  • p = frictional resistance (psi/ft of pipe)
  • Q = flow in gallons per minute (gpm)
  • C = Hazen-Williams roughness coefficient
  • d = actual internal pipe diameter (inches)

Total pressure loss per segment = p × pipe length (incl. fitting equivalents).

What the Math Tells You

  • Flow term: Q¹·⁸⁵ — double the flow = 3.6× the loss
  • Diameter term: d⁴·⁸⁷ — go up one pipe size = 6× LESS loss
  • C term: dirty/dry pipe (lower C) = more loss

Lesson: bumping a single segment one pipe size often costs less than the extra pump pressure to push through a smaller one.

Worked snippet

10 ft of 1-in. steel wet pipe (d ≈ 1.049", C = 120) carrying 25 gpm:
p = 4.52 × 25¹·⁸⁵ / (120¹·⁸⁵ × 1.049⁴·⁸⁷) = 4.52 × 397 / (8,940 × 1.26) ≈ 0.16 psi/ft
Over 10 ft → ~1.6 psi loss. Try the same flow in 2-in. pipe (d=2.067): loss drops to ~0.06 psi/ft. Bigger pipe = dramatic loss reduction.

Elevation & Junction Balance

Two often-skipped pressure adjustments

Elevation Pressure (§27.2.4.8.1)

Add or subtract 0.433 psi per foot of elevation change between nodes.

ΔP_elev = 0.433 × Δh (psi/ft × ft)
  • Going UP the system → ADD 0.433 psi/ft
  • Going DOWN the system → SUBTRACT 0.433 psi/ft

A 30-ft elevation rise from grade to roof = 13.0 psi of pressure used up just to lift the water.

Pressure Balance at Junctions (§27.2.2.4.1)

At every hydraulic junction, pressures arriving from different paths must balance within 0.5 psi (0.03 bar).

If two paths converge with mismatched pressure:

  • Use the highest pressure (carries forward conservatively)
  • Adjust the flow in the higher-pressure path UP using K = Q/√P relationship
  • Recalculate until both paths agree within 0.5 psi

Why the 0.5 psi tolerance

Hydraulic software iterates until junctions balance. The 0.5 psi tolerance is large enough to be achievable in finite iterations but small enough that the resulting design is accurate. Hand calcs sometimes show 1+ psi imbalance — that's a red flag for the reviewer.

The Setup

Plot the water supply curve and the system demand point on a special semi-exponential graph paper.

  • Show the AHJ a clean pass/fail visual

  • Electric or diesel driver
  • Sized at: rated flow, rated psi, 150% capacity

② Water Tank (NFPA 22)

Used when the supply has insufficient flow regardless of pressure. The tank stores water; gravity or pump delivers it.

  • Volume = demand flow × duration
  • Common in rural or industrial sites
  • Elevated tank → gravity head
  • Ground tank → pump from tank to riser

③ Tank + Pump Combo

When you need both flow AND pressure. Most common for high-hazard or remote sites.

  • Suction tank dedicated to fire service
  • Fire pump pulls from tank
  • Tank refilled by lower-flow city service
  • Most expensive but most reliable

Quick Decision Tree

Supply flow OK, pressure too low? → Fire pump.
Supply flow too low? → Tank for storage volume, possibly + pump.
Supply unreliable / not available? → Tank + pump combo, dedicated fire service.

Always size the pump so its rated point lies at or above the system demand on the supply graph.

Pt. 5 — Common Field Mistakes

Real failures designers make on Ch. 19 / Ch. 27 work

① Forgetting fitting equivalent lengths

10 ft of pipe + a 90° ell at 1-in. = 12 ft equivalent. Skip the ell and your friction loss is 20% low. Calculation passes; reality fails.

② Picking the WRONG density/area point

Choosing 0.10 gpm/ft² over 3,900 ft² when you could've chosen 0.15 over 1,500 ft² makes pipes BIGGER, not smaller. Try both — the math doesn't intuit.

③ Using a wet C-value on a dry system

C=120 vs C=100 — a 20-point error. Friction loss off by ~30%. Software defaults catch this; hand calcs often don't.

④ Skipping the elevation term

0.433 psi/ft adds up fast. 30 ft to a roof = 13 psi. Missing this passes the wrong design.

⑤ QR reduction without all 4 conditions

Used QR sprinklers on a dry system, or on EH, or with ceiling 22 ft. None qualify — reduction is invalid.

⑥ Flow test >12 months without AHJ approval

§4.6.1.1 cap is hard. Don't assume the AHJ will look the other way. Get a new test or get a written waiver.

⑦ Forgetting the dry pipe area increase

§19.3.3.2.5 mandates +30% area for dry systems. Forgetting it means under-designing the most fire-prone systems (the dry ones).

Prevention Discipline

Build a review checklist for every calc submittal that explicitly hits each of the above. Trust software, but verify the inputs — software only computes what you feed it.
