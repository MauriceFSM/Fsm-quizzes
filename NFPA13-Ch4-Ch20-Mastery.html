What is NFPA 13?

Knowing which standard you're under is rule #1

NFPA 13

Standard for the Installation of Sprinkler Systems

Commercial, industrial, institutional, high-rise residential. Covers all occupancies not exclusive to 13R/13D.

★ This is the standard this course covers.

NFPA 13R

Residential up to 4 stories above grade plane.

Lower-cost system tailored to dwelling-unit fire control, not full property protection. Garages, attics, and concealed spaces often omitted.

NFPA 13D

One- and two-family dwellings and manufactured homes.

Life-safety focus. Smallest pipe, simplest design. Frequently uses domestic water supply (multipurpose).

Why this matters

Picking the wrong standard means your design is non-compliant before you draw a single pipe. A 5-story apartment isn't 13R territory (over 4 stories = NFPA 13). A warehouse never falls under 13R/13D.

Level of Protection

§4.1 — The first decision before any other

The Core Rule

A building protected by an automatic sprinkler system shall have sprinklers in ALL areas — with only the specific omissions this standard permits.

There is no "partial protection by default." Every square foot must be accounted for.

Limited Area Systems

When partial systems are installed:

  • NFPA 13 requirements still apply "insofar as applicable"
  • Must consult the AHJ on a case-by-case basis
  • Common in tenant fit-outs and storage rooms in otherwise non-sprinklered shells

What Chapter 4 Defines

Who is responsible. What info must be provided. Where sprinklers must go.

No design work begins without first determining hazard classification and coverage requirements.

Mental model: Ch. 4 is the project intake — what you must KNOW before you draw. Ch. 19 is the design method — HOW you draw. Ch. 20+ is WHAT you draw for storage.

MODULE 2 · §9.3 OMISSIONSSLIDE 07 / 61FSM

Permitted Omissions

§4.1.1 references §9.3 — the actual list of where sprinklers may be omitted

Concealed Spaces

  • Noncombustible/limited-combustible spaces with minimal combustible loading (§9.3.1)
  • Spaces filled with noncombustible insulation (§9.3.4)
  • Wood-joist concealed spaces meeting volume and area limits (§9.3.6)
  • Spaces above lay-in ceilings that are noncombustible and not used for storage

Small Spaces

  • Bathrooms ≤ 55 ft² in dwelling units of Light Hazard occupancies (§9.3.17)
  • Closets ≤ 24 ft² with least dimension ≤ 3 ft in dwelling units
  • Vestibules and similar small noncombustible spaces with no combustibles

Special Areas

  • Electrical rooms ≤ 2 hr fire-rated, dedicated, dry-type equipment only (§9.3.10)
  • Elevator machine rooms with conditions (§9.3.9 — coordinate with NFPA 13/72/elevator code)
  • Walk-in freezers protected with dry/preaction or alternative (§9.3.12)
  • Open parking structures meeting building-code openness criteria

The Big Caveat

Each omission has specific conditions. "Concealed space" alone is not enough — the construction type, combustible loading, ventilation, and use of the space all matter.

Always cite the exact §9.3 subsection when you tell a GC "no sprinklers here." If you can't cite it, install the sprinkler.

MODULE 2 · §4.2SLIDE 08 / 61FSM

The Owner's Certificate

§4.2 — Required before any design work begins

What the Owner Must Provide

  1. Intended use of the building — materials stored, maximum storage height
  2. Preliminary plan of the building with design concepts for sprinkler layout
  3. Water supply information as identified in §5.2.2 (now §4.6)
  4. Special water supply knowledge — environmental conditions, corrosion risks, MIC (Microbiologically Influenced Corrosion)

Why This Comes First

Submitted to the sprinkler system installer prior to layout and detailing.

Without these 4 items, the designer cannot:

  • Classify the occupancy hazard
  • Size the water supply
  • Determine sprinkler type and placement

Real world

If the owner's cert is incomplete or unsigned, the design is invalid. Always get it in writing before drawing a single pipe.

Mnemonic — "U-P-W-S": Use · Plan · Water · Special conditions. Four inputs, no work without them.

Occupancy Hazard Classifications

§4.3 — Five tiers of fire risk that drive design density & water supply
LHLight Hazard — low quantity & combustibility
OH1Ordinary Hazard Gp 1 — moderate qty, low combustibility
OH2Ordinary Hazard Gp 2 — moderate-high qty & combustibility
EH1Extra Hazard Gp 1 — very high combustibility, rapid fire
EH2Extra Hazard Gp 2 — flammable liquids OR extensive shielding
Key rule (§4.3.1.1–4.3.1.2): Classifications relate to sprinkler design & water supply ONLY. They are NOT a general occupancy hazard rating, NOT a code-enforcement label.

What Drives the Class

Three variables, judged together:

  1. Quantity of combustibles per unit area (fire load)
  2. Combustibility — how easily it ignites, how fast it spreads
  3. Rate of heat release — does the fire grow slowly or explosively?

Each class has a matching design density (gpm/ft²) and area (ft²) on the density/area curves (Ch. 19).

Why this matters

Misclassify down (say OH1 when you should be OH2) and you under-design. Misclassify up and the owner pays for capacity they never need.

Tie-Breaker Rules

  • If any portion of a space meets a higher hazard, the higher hazard governs that portion (§4.5.3)

  • The AHJ has final say when borderline

Most Common Mistake

Calling an auto repair shop "OH1" because cars are "mostly metal." The tires, fluids, plastics, and shop combustibles push it to OH2 (per the §4.3.4 list).

Worked Example — Classify 5 Occupancies

Stop, think, then check your answer against the analysis

① Suburban Dental Office

2,500 ft² office build-out. Operatories with dental chairs, sterilizer, X-ray room, small lab.

→ LH — low fuel load, low combustibility, mostly metal/glass/finishes. Slow HRR expected.

② Indoor Parking Garage (commercial)

Three levels, ~120 cars typical. No fueling. Concrete deck on concrete columns.

→ OH1 — §4.3.3 explicitly lists auto parking garages. Cars contain combustibles but moderate qty, low-to-mod HRR.

③ Print Shop — Heat-Set Web Offset

Commercial printer, rolls of paper, ink/solvent feed, oven for ink cure.

→ EH1 — §4.3.5 lists "printing (heatset web offset)" by name. Rapid fire potential from web speed + ink solvents.

④ Auto Body Spray Booth Area

Open spray booths using flammable solvent-based paints. Drying area adjacent.

→ EH2 — §4.3.6 names "flammable liquids spraying." Liquids fueling rapid fire growth = top tier.

⑤ Distribution Warehouse, Cardboard Boxes Stored 18 ft

Cartoned small electronics, rack storage, 18 ft high.

→ NOT a §4.3 occupancy hazard. Primary use = storage above misc-storage thresholds. Apply Chapter 20, then branch to Ch. 21/22/23 for sprinkler design.

Pattern: If "things are kept here for later use" is the building's purpose → storage chapter. If "things happen here" (work, dwelling, gathering) → occupancy hazard.

Miscellaneous & Low-Piled Storage

§4.3.1.4 – 4.3.1.8 — When small storage stays under Ch. 4 instead of Ch. 20

Miscellaneous Storage Limits (§4.3.1.4)

  • Must NOT exceed 10% of building area OR 4,000 ft² (whichever is greater) of sprinklered area
  • No single pile or area > 1,000 ft² (93 m²)
  • Must be separated from other piles by at least 25 ft (7.6 m)

Low-Piled Key Thresholds (§4.3.1.5)

  • Class I–IV at ≤ 12 ft on solid shelf racks → in-rack sprinklers per §25.6.1 + ceiling protection
  • Group A plastics at ≤ 5 ft on solid shelf racks → same rule applies
  • Max design area for misc/low-piled: 3,000 ft² (279 m²) — except tire storage

Tire Storage Special Rule

Miscellaneous tire piles limited to 2,000 ft² and pile width ≤ 25 ft in the direction of wheel holes (§4.3.1.6).

Why "Miscellaneous" Exists

Real buildings have a back room with a few pallets of paper or a small parts cage. Forcing those into full Ch. 20 protection would be wasteful.

§4.3.1 is the relief valve — if your storage fits within the three limits above, you can stay under Ch. 4 occupancy design and just add some hose/duration per Table 4.3.1.7.1.

3-rule check: 10%/4k total · 1k per pile · 25 ft separation. If any one fails, go to Ch. 20.

Example

50,000 ft² office building wants 3 pallets of copy paper in a 600 ft² supply room. 10% of building = 5,000 ft² (more than 4,000) and 600 < 1,000 — eligible as misc storage if separation is met.

Working Plans

§4.4 — What must appear on submitted drawings

Plan Information (§4.4)

  • Name & address of owner, occupant, contractor
  • Scale of drawings (typical ⅛" or ¼" = 1'-0")
  • North arrow + key plan
  • Full dimensions of building/area, ceiling construction, slopes
  • Type of sprinklers (K-factor, temperature, response, orientation)
  • Piping sizes, lengths, fitting count
  • Hanger types, locations, pipe support details
  • Hydraulic calc summary node / reference point
  • Hazard classification and design density/area
  • Water supply source, flow test data, assumptions

Calculations Submittal

Hydraulic calculations must accompany the drawings and include:

  • Summary sheet with system identifier
  • Detailed nodes, flows, pressures
  • Graph plot — supply vs demand curve
  • K-factor selection rationale
  • Hose stream allowance

If You Omit Any of This

AHJ reject. Project schedule slips. Worse: your stamp is on an incomplete record. NFPA 13 §4.4 is the AHJ's checklist — match it line-for-line.

Pro tip: Many AHJs publish their own plan-review checklist that mirrors §4.4. Pull it before submittal.

§4.5 — Maximum floor area per single sprinkler system / riser

Per Hazard Type

Hazard TypeMax Area (per system)

These limits apply per riser / per single system. Bigger buildings = more risers.

What Counts in the Area?

  • All sprinklered floor area served by that system
  • Multiple connected floors of LH/OH count cumulatively, unless feeding through approved isolation
  • Mezzanines have their own rules (next slide)
  • Combined LH/OH areas can total 52,000 ft²

Why the Limit Exists

If one system fires, the whole system drains. Bigger systems = longer fill / pressure-loss / drain-down = worse fire response. 52,000 / 40,000 balances economy and reliability.

Real-world

A 90,000 ft² LH office can't be one system — must be split with detached risers / floor-control valves. Plan riser locations early.

Mezzanine Rules

§4.5.2 — When mezzanine area counts toward your 52,000/40,000 cap

Scenario 1 — Single System

One system covers the main floor AND the open mezzanine above.

✓ Mezzanine area NOT counted toward §4.5.1 limit.

The mezzanine sprinklers are "free area" because they're on the same demand.

Scenario 2 — Multi-System, Same Boundary

Multiple systems share a common boundary, mezzanine fully within one system's coverage.

✓ Mezzanine area NOT counted.

Contained within one system's vertical column.

Scenario 3 — Different System Feeds Mezzanine

The mezzanine is fed by a different system than the floor below it.

✗ Mezzanine area ADDED to supplying system — must still meet §4.5.1.

Common gotcha in multi-tenant fit-outs.

Worked check

A 50,000 ft² OH1 ground floor has a 4,000 ft² open mezzanine. If one system feeds both: total stays at 50,000 — fine. If a separate mezzanine system: that mezzanine system sees only 4,000 ft² — also fine, well under 52,000.

Mixed Hazard Systems

§4.5.3 — Combining different hazards on one system

The Two Rules

  1. The portion designed to the higher hazard shall not exceed its own area limit (EH portion ≤ 40,000 ft²).
  2. The total combined system shall not exceed the largest applicable limit (52,000 ft²).
Plain English: The EH zone obeys EH rules. The OH zone obeys OH rules. The sum doesn't get bigger because you mixed.

Reading the Three Numbers

Why Storage Needs Its Own Chapter

Where Chapter 4 occupancy classification stops working

Ch. 4's Blind Spots

Occupancy hazard works fine for offices, garages, factories. It breaks down when:

  • Storage HEIGHT dominates fire behavior — a 25-ft pile burns very differently than a 6-ft pile of the same product
  • Packaging changes fuel: a cardboard box vs the plastic bin holding the same widget
  • Pallet material is itself fuel — plastic pallets shift the entire fire curve
  • Rack flue spaces create vertical chimneys you can't model with floor-area density
  • Shielding by upper rack levels prevents sprinkler spray from reaching lower fires

What Ch. 20 Adds

  • Commodity classes I, II, III, IV + Group A/B/C plastics — fuel taxonomy
  • Storage methods — solid pile, palletized, rack types
  • Height & clearance rules — storage height vs ceiling matters
  • Idle pallet rules — your "empty" pallets are fuel
  • Column protection — structural steel needs cooling, not just suppression
  • Mixed commodity rules — how the worst pallet drives the whole rack
Ch. 4 vs Ch. 20: Ch. 4 = WHAT the building is used for. Ch. 20 = WHAT & HOW stuff is stored. Storage is its own physics.

Concrete case

Same product — cartoned canned goods — protects very differently if it's on shelves in a grocery aisle (LH/OH1 territory) vs 24 ft high in a distribution rack (Ch. 20, possibly EH-equivalent with K-25 ESFR ceiling sprinklers).

K-Factor — What It Means

A primer before Ch. 20 starts throwing K-numbers at you

The Formula

Q = K √P

  • Q = flow in gpm (gallons per minute)
  • K = sprinkler discharge coefficient (the K-factor)
  • P = pressure at the sprinkler in psi

A bigger K means more flow at the same pressure. Or the same flow at a lower pressure. Bigger orifice = bigger K.

K-Factor Cheat Sheet

K (US)K (metric)Typical Use
K-5.6K-80Standard LH/OH ceiling — most common
K-8.0K-115OH2, light storage
K-11.2K-160Storage, CMSA
K-14.0K-200Storage, ESFR
K-16.8K-240ESFR, high-challenge storage
K-22.4 / 25.2K-320 / K-360ESFR, tallest/most demanding storage

Why bigger K matters in storage

A K-14 ESFR at 50 psi delivers ~99 gpm per sprinkler. A K-5.6 at the same 50 psi only gives ~40 gpm. To put out a tall stack fire, you need fewer but bigger sprinklers driving a heavy spray that can punch through the thermal plume.

MODULE 4 · SPRINKLER TYPESSLIDE 33 / 61FSM

Sprinkler Families

CMDA · CMSA · ESFR · In-Rack — the four storage tools

CMDA — Control Mode Density / Area (Ch. 21)

Traditional approach: deliver a density (gpm/ft²) over a design area (ft²). Most flexible — works for nearly any commodity / configuration if you size it.

Trade-off: requires more sprinklers operating simultaneously than ESFR.

CMSA — Control Mode Specific Application (Ch. 22)

Specific listed sprinklers (typically K-11.2 / K-14) at fixed flow per sprinkler over a specific number of design sprinklers. Larger orifice, larger droplets, better cooling.

Often called the "old large drop" approach.

ESFR — Early Suppression Fast Response (Ch. 23)

Goal isn't control — it's suppression. Fast-response thermal element + high-momentum spray that penetrates the fire plume before it grows.

Strict installation rules — ceiling slope, clearance, obstructions all limited.

In-Rack (Ch. 25)

Sprinklers inside the rack flue spaces or transverse aisles. Used when ceiling protection alone can't reach lower-rack fires.

Always paired with ceiling protection — never replaces it.

How to choose: ESFR is fastest & cleanest when the building can host it (slopes, ceiling height, clearance). CMDA / CMSA when ESFR doesn't fit. In-rack when ceiling-only can't suppress.

Commodity Classification Rules

§20.3 — How to assign a class to a real product

What You Must Consider (§20.3.1.1)

  1. Type & amount of product materials — the product itself
  2. Type & amount of primary packaging — the carton or container
  3. Type of pallet — wood, metal, plastic (reinforced or not)

All three together set the class. Two products that look identical in a catalog can be different classes because of pallet or packaging changes.

Three-Step Classification

  1. Identify base class from §20.4 — what is the product itself + packaging?
  2. Apply pallet upclass per §20.3.2 — wood/metal = no change, plastic = +1 or +2
  3. Check mixed commodity rule §20.3.3 — if mixed, highest class governs unless <10% exception applies

Conservatism

When in doubt, classify higher. The cost of one extra class in design is small. The cost of under-protection is a building.

The "Outside the List" Problem

Real warehouses store products NFPA 13 doesn't list by name. Apply the three-step framework, document your reasoning, and confirm with the AHJ. Never guess silently — your assumption goes on the drawings.

Commodity Classes I–IV

§20.4 — The fuel taxonomy that drives every storage design

Class I (§20.4.1)

Noncombustible products on wood pallets, OR in single-layer corrugated cartons with/without cardboard dividers, OR shrink-wrapped/paper-wrapped as a unit load.

Class II (§20.4.2)

Noncombustible products in slatted wooden crates, solid wood boxes, multiple-layered corrugated cartons, or equivalent combustible packaging.

Class III (§20.4.3)

Products fashioned from wood, paper, natural fibers, or Group C plastics — with or without cartons/pallets. May contain ≤ 5% Group A plastic (by weight nonexpanded OR by volume expanded).

Class IV (§20.4.4)

Class I–III products containing an appreciable amount of Group A plastic in cartoned or expanded form, OR products of Group B plastics, OR products of free-flowing Group A plastic.

Hazard direction: Class I → Class IV = increasing fire load. Class IV is the highest of the I–IV ladder. Plastics (Group A unexposed/exposed) sit ABOVE Class IV.

Group A, B & C Plastics

§20.4.5 – 20.4.7 — Plastic commodity sub-classes

Group A — Highest Fire Hazard

Plastics not classified as Group B or C. Includes:

ABS, acrylic, fiberglass (FRP), nylon, PET, polycarbonate, polypropylene, polystyrene, polyurethane (foam & non-foam), PVC (rigid), PVDC.

Sub-types:

  • Expanded (foam) — most severe
  • Nonexpanded (solid) — moderately severe
  • Cartoned vs Exposed affects design dramatically

Group B — Moderate

Less flammable than A but more than C:

Cellulosics (acetate, butyrate, propionate), chloroprene rubber, natural & nitrile rubber (if expanded), flexible PVC.

Key facts:

  • Still requires elevated protection
  • Does NOT trigger Group A plastic rules
  • Used in Class IV commodity classification

Group C — Lowest

Plastics that burn slowly with limited fire propagation:

Fluoroplastics (PTFE, ETFE), melamine, phenolic, polyvinyl chloride (rigid, heavy).

Key facts:

  • Treated like wood/paper → Class III
  • Does NOT elevate commodity class
  • Most fire-resistant plastic group

The Big One

Group A expanded plastic (foam) is the most demanding commodity NFPA 13 protects. It is what drives ESFR K-25 designs and in-rack sprinklers in high-pile storage.

MODULE 4 · §20.3.3SLIDE 40 / 61FSM

Mixed Commodities

§20.3.3 — When one rack holds different classes

The Default Rule

When commodities of different classes are stored together, design protection for the highest class present in any rack area.

Why: any pallet of the higher class can start a worst-case fire. The whole rack sees that demand.

The "≤10% Exception" (§20.3.3.2)

You may design to the lower class if ALL of these are true:

  • The higher-class commodity occupies ≤ 10% of any 40,000 ft² rack area
  • The higher-class commodity does NOT exceed 10 pallet loads
  • The higher-class is randomly distributed (not concentrated in one zone)
  • You document it on the drawings AND control it operationally

Operational Reality

The exception is administrative. It requires the owner to control where the higher-class goes. The moment a forklift driver puts 11 plastic-pallet loads in one bay, your design is non-compliant. Most real designs ignore the exception and just design to the highest class — simpler, more robust, no operational dependency.

Rule of thumb: Design to the worst pallet in the rack. The 10% exception is a paper saving, not an engineering saving.

Storage Methods

§20.5 — How stuff sits on the floor or in the rack drives sprinkler choice

Solid-Piled

Product stacked directly, no pallets, no racks. Bales of cardboard, drums on the floor.

No flue spaces — fire ventilation differs from racks. Often requires higher density.

Palletized

Product on pallets on the floor (no rack structure). Stacked directly pallet-on-pallet typical.

Pallet upclass rules apply. Height limited by stacking stability.

Bin Box & Shelf

Bin Box: open-top containers on shelves or floor.
Shelf: flat shelf, less than 30" deep typically.

Shelf storage triggers in-rack rules above 12 ft Class I-IV (§4.3.1.5).

Single-Row Rack

One pallet deep. Forklift access from one side only. Transverse flue every 8–10 ft.

Easiest rack to protect — flues are wide open.

Double-Row Rack

Two pallets deep, accessed from both sides. Shared longitudinal flue space between rows.

The longitudinal flue is critical for in-rack sprinkler placement.

Multi-Row Rack

3+ rows deep, limited aisle access. Includes drive-in, drive-through, push-back, flow-through.

Hardest to protect — sprinkler spray can't reach interior. Often requires in-rack at multiple levels.

Why Method Matters

Same commodity, same height, same building → very different sprinkler systems based on storage method. Single-row of Class IV at 20 ft ≠ drive-in rack of Class IV at 20 ft. The drive-in needs in-rack sprinklers; the single-row might be fine with ESFR ceiling-only.

Flue Definitions

  • Transverse flue — the side-to-side gap between adjacent pallets in the same row. Travels vertically up the rack. Typical minimum: 3".
  • Longitudinal flue — the back-to-back gap between the two rows of a double-row rack. Travels vertically. Typical minimum: 6".

Why Flues Save Lives

Hot gases rise through flues, triggering sprinklers near the fire. Water also falls down through flues to reach lower pallets.

Block a flue with a pallet sticking out and the rack becomes a closed-box chimney. Fire grows; spray can't reach.

Field check: Walk the aisle. You should see vertical light gaps between every pallet face and at the row-to-row centerline. If you can't, flues are blocked.

Heights & Clearances

§20.6 — The vertical geometry that drives sprinkler selection

The Three Heights You Track

  • Storage Height — top of the highest pallet to the floor. The number used to look up design tables.
  • Building Height — floor to the underside of the roof deck (NOT to the ridge).
  • Clearance — distance from top of storage to the sprinkler deflector.

Why deck-not-ridge

Deck = where sprinklers and spray live. Ridge is structure. Calculations are deck-relative.

The 18" Minimum Clearance (§8.6.6 / §20.6)

A minimum 18" (457 mm) clearance must be maintained between the top of storage and the sprinkler deflectors.

Less clearance = spray pattern can't form. The sprinkler's "umbrella" has no room to open.

ESFR Clearance Rules

ESFR ceilings have specific tables (Ch. 23) for max ceiling height vs storage height vs K-factor. Examples:

  • K-14.0 ESFR: 25 ft storage → 30 ft ceiling, 50 psi
  • K-16.8 ESFR: 25 ft storage → 32 ft ceiling, 60 psi
  • K-22.4 ESFR: up to 45 ft storage → 48 ft ceiling
Field reality: 18" sounds like a lot until you see a forklift driver overshoot. Always design with a buffer — call out a 24" effective clearance to absorb operations.

Adjacent Hazards & Building Construction

§20.7 & §20.8 — Context the storage area lives inside

§20.7 — Adjacent Hazards

When storage shares a building with other hazards:

  • The storage system protects the storage area + 25 ft buffer into adjacent space
  • The adjacent occupancy gets its own system if it triggers Ch. 4 hazard rules independently
  • At the boundary, the higher demand governs

A 35-ft Class IV storage area next to a Light Hazard office: design the boundary 25 ft into the office to storage criteria.

§20.8 — Building Construction

Building construction (combustible vs noncombustible deck, truss type, ceiling height variations) affects:

  • Whether obstruction rules apply to bottom-chord of trusses
  • Need for noncombustible draft curtains around large openings
  • Sprinkler orientation (upright vs pendant) where deck is combustible

Combustible wood-deck overhead = sprinklers must be close to deck (within 1–6") to wet structure during fire.

Real-World Trap

The architect adds a "small" wood mezzanine above a storage area to fix a square-footage shortage. Now the storage system has a combustible obstruction overhead that drastically changes sprinkler layout. Catch this at design, not at the inspection.

Common Protection Rules

§20.9 – §20.12 — The "small print" that applies to every storage design

§20.9 — Discharge Considerations

All storage sprinkler systems must include hose-stream and water-supply duration per the applicable design table (Ch. 21–25).

  • Hose typically 250 or 500 gpm
  • Duration 60 to 120 minutes depending on hazard
  • Hose is in addition to sprinkler demand

§20.10 — Hose Connections

Inside hose stations or standpipe outlets required when required by the building code OR when the AHJ deems necessary for manual fire fighting access.

§20.11 — Separation Between Storage Areas

When two different storage configurations share a building:

  • Design each area to its own criteria
  • Maintain physical separation OR design the boundary to the higher criteria
  • "Soft" separation (painted line) is not separation — must be physical

§20.12 — Aisles

Minimum aisle widths matter for sprinkler coverage and fire-department access. Typical: ≥ 8 ft between racks for standard storage; narrower aisles trigger more in-rack sprinklers.

Tighter aisles ≠ cheaper protection. Often the opposite.

MODULE 4 · §20.13 – §20.14 (wood)SLIDE 48 / 61FSM

Idle Wood Pallets

§20.13 Combustible Loading & §20.14 — Empty pallets are fuel

The Problem

A stack of empty wood pallets is one of the worst fire loads in any warehouse — high surface area, air gaps acting as flue, dry wood, vertical stack feeding rapid spread.

Treating empty pallets like "not real storage" is how warehouses burn down.

§20.14.1 — Wood Pallet Storage Limits

Idle wood pallets stored indoors:

  • Max 6 ft high in piles unprotected — increase if sprinkler design accommodates
  • 50 pallets per pile default limit without special protection
  • Piles separated by ≥ 8 ft OR 25 ft of clear space / commodity
  • Treated as separate storage zone, NOT misc storage

Outdoor Storage

Outdoor idle pallets must be separated from buildings/lot lines per §20.14.1.2 (typically ≥ 50 ft from buildings, more for larger piles).

Best Practice

  • Dedicate a zone for idle pallets — don't scatter them through the racks
  • Bundle & tie piles for stability
  • Inventory rotation — don't let pallets accumulate beyond design assumptions
  • Train forklift operators on the rule — they're the ones who stack them

Trigger (§20.15.1)

Column protection is required when BOTH:

  • A structural column is within the rack footprint OR within 12 in. of the rack footprint

  • Sidewall sprinkler at 15 ft directing spray at column

Pt. 5 — Common Field Mistakes

Real failures designers make on the Acme-style project

① "Just design to Class IV — it's only 5% plastic"

Treating the §20.3.3 exception as a default. Owner changes SKUs, exception fails, system under-designed. Design for the worst plausible.

② Pallet type assumed from a phone call

"Owner said wood pallets" → drawings show wood → operations buys reinforced plastic on sale. The class jumps two. Get it on the owner's cert in writing.

③ Forgetting idle pallet storage

The 200 empty pallets piled in the back corner are not in the design. First fire inspection flags them. Project halts.

④ Mezzanine area added to system without §4.5.2 check

The 5,000 ft² office mezzanine is fed by a separate system but boundary not declared. §4.5.2 Scenario 3 trap.

⑤ ESFR slope/obstruction not field-verified

The drawings show clean deck. The roof has a 1:12 slope and HVAC duct work. ESFR listings have strict limits — often missed at design stage.

⑥ Flue space not enforced

Forklift drivers crowd pallets to fit more inventory. Transverse flues drop to 1". In-rack rules now triggered AFTER the system is installed.

How to Prevent

  • Operational MOU with the owner — pallet rules in writing
  • Annual ITM walkthrough includes flue inspection
  • Drawings show pallet type & commodity assumption explicitly
